Philosophy & Mission

Engineering Philosophy

Are we in discovery, or are we just learning fast? Speed and overhead are always in tension — both have a cost, and getting the balance wrong wastes more than any bug ever will. Great engineering means owning that call: reading the situation, calibrating the approach, and driving to completion. Not a fixed set of beliefs. A practice that's always being refined.

Mission

To build systems that better humanity at scale — by diagnosing the right problems, defining the change required, and driving it to completion.

What I'm Focused On

Hard-tech. Non-assault defense. Aerospace — including space-based manufacturing. Systems that create genuine leverage, not just economic output. The good side of the blade.

Looking For

Roles where I'm expected to own the solution — not execute a ticket. Give me a big problem, a clear outcome, and the trust to break it down, build the plan, and drive it across the finish line. Best under pressure. Best with a hard problem. Best alongside a team that takes the work seriously. If you need someone to uplevel the work around them — that's the job.

Working with me

Strengths
Grit
Absorbs more than most. When people are looking for the exit, I'm looking for the fix. Long hours, hard clients, failing systems — without making it a personality trait.
Insightful
Fast at identifying the actual problem — technical, process, or people. Multiple senior engineers have noted this independently, regardless of experience level.
PM-Conscious
Constantly evaluating the strategic "why" behind every task. Not just executing — thinking about what should be executed and whether the framing is right.
Versatility
Switches between deep technical execution and open strategic thinking without friction. Wide toolkit — uses all of it.
Fast on My Feet
Real-time thinking under pressure: live debugging in front of clients, path-finding when the plan breaks, staying useful when the situation changes.
Communication
Structured, direct, keeps the written record. Pulls others into the conversation. Not a soft skill — a technical advantage on complex projects.
Watch-Outs
Don't Confuse Confidence With Coverage
My independence and quality output can make it look like I don't need oversight. I do. Don't let my confidence lull you out of your management responsibilities — you're still responsible for your role. Multiple eyes on big projects matter.
Tunnel Vision
The closer I am to the critical path, the more narrowly I focus. That's a feature until it's a bug. Hard to maintain perspective when deep in it — this is as true for me as most. Call in the second set of eyes before it's needed.
Pushing Past the Signal
I'll take more pressure than most. That sometimes means ignoring signals to step back and pushing through until stopping is not an option. If you see it coming before I do, say so directly.
Eager to Answer, Sometimes Too Early
My instinct is to engage fast and puzzle things out in real-time. That's usually an asset — but when new to a project, I can speak before I have full context. In high-stakes client settings especially: pull me aside if you see me speculating without the full picture.
How to Collaborate
Build in the challenge
Confidence and quality output can make it look like due diligence isn't needed. It is. I'm often capable of being both confident AND correct — which makes systematic challenge more important, not less. Don't let the results talk you out of your process.
Involve me in the full process
I want to be part of problem definition, solution framing, execution, and iteration — not just handed the execution phase. That's where I use my full skill set and where things don't get missed.
Bring me in early
I'm a thought partner, not a ticket-taker. Let me help define the problem, not just execute the solution that's already been scoped.
Pace me
If you see me prioritizing speed over stability, say so. It's always welcome. I'd rather be told to slow down than discover I needed to.
